Shoo fly, don't bother me

"the tools / limits inform the process"

Identifying distractions in my workflow is something I've always struggled with. The one distraction that seems to always, at least initially, block me is the presence of a computer. Whether it's a notification to check my email (or whatever), an OS update, or just the uinspiring glare of the mouse and keyboard as my day job forces me behind a computer for much of the workday.

the tools / config

Less is more

I like to chunk my studio time in achievable goals or sessions whereby I may or may not include the computer. I've done a variety of things to remove the computer, or friction, but I always find it helpful towards the end of especially to flesh out the song as a whole. It's at the initial phase(s) of building a song that I want to hide the computer. Once it's gone, the assortment of choices to pluck dwindles greatly and the possibilities become much easier to execute.

Hey fly, come back here

Though, when a computer really comes in handy for me is when there's an idea ripe for the plucking. A skeleton just waiting to be filled with embellished guts of context. It's at this point that the computer becomes indispensible.

Overdoing it

Part of the reasoning behind removing the computer is to set some limits on a song finding its footing. And even within' those limitations, the possibilities are still quite limitless. I've gone to further lengths to limit what I could do when starting a song like creating "limitation" cards that essentially set parameters for each track in a song. I have a feeling it is at times like these that I have gone too far and made the process far too rigid / inflexible.

limitation cards (too far ?)

Go fast

Throughout all of this I have discovered that the config / limits imposed are what inform the process, and therefore, the ideas / creations that grow from them. - remove blockers from the get-go - know your tools intimately - get started with a plan (or don't :o)
